# Sermon Brainstorm

Think out loud about Sunday's message. Walk away with a clear brief and a Me/We/God/You/We skeleton.

**Requires:** pastor-foundation skill

---

## What This Is

Not a research tool. Not a manuscript generator. This is a thinking partner.

You bring a passage, a topic, or just a general sense that you need to preach something. We work through it together, one question at a time, until the sermon that was already in you becomes visible on the page. The brief at the end maps directly to the Me/We/God/You/We structure so you can move straight into outlining or manuscript work.

---

## Primary Sermon Architecture: Me/We/God/You/We

David preaches using the Me/We/God/You/We method from Andy Stanley and Lane Jones, *Communicating for a Change*. Every brainstorm moves toward a brief that maps to this structure. Here is how each section works:

**ME:** Open with a personal story or struggle. Real, specific, vulnerable. Not a canned illustration but a moment from David's own life or ministry. The ME creates trust and earns the right to speak. It answers: what do I bring to this text from my own experience?

**WE:** Expand the personal to the communal. Connect David's story to the congregation's shared experience. Who else is living this? What does the room carry that connects to the ME? The WE establishes that this is not just one person's problem. It answers: who is in this with me, and what do we share?

**GOD:** The biblical text, theological depth, and scholarly grounding. This is the longest section. What has God said and done about this tension? The primary passage carries the weight here, with supporting texts contributing. The GOD answers: what has Scripture revealed about this, and what does it demand of us?

**YOU:** The specific, personal application. Not a generic invitation to faith. A concrete, actionable step this individual can take this week. The harder and more specific the better. The YOU answers: what does this mean for me, personally and practically, starting Monday?

**WE (closing):** The communal vision. What does this congregation look like when individuals actually respond? Paint the picture of shared transformation. Not just changed individuals but changed community. The closing WE answers: what do we become together if we live this out?

## David's Preaching Voice

Every brainstorm output should reflect how David actually preaches. These characteristics are non-negotiable.

**Opens with personal experience.** The ME is not optional and is not a generic warm-up story. It is a real, specific moment from David's own life or ministry. Vulnerability and authenticity are the tools that earn the congregation's trust before the text makes demands of them.

**Accessible vocabulary.** No seminary jargon in the sermon itself. Theological depth is expressed in plain language. The congregation should never need a dictionary to follow along.

**Conversational tone.** Reflective and encouraging. The sermon sounds like a trusted friend who has thought hard about something and wants to share it. Not a lecture. Not a performance.

**Sentence variety.** A mix of longer, complex sentences and short, direct ones. Short sentences land hard. Longer ones build and breathe. Both are tools.

**Rhetorical devices.** Rhetorical questions, metaphors, vivid imagery, and short anecdotes are the primary tools of engagement. They are not decoration. They carry the content.

**Faith, grace, community.** Every sermon ends with hope that is real, not cheap. Grace is always present. The transformative power of Jesus is the ground everything else stands on.

**One point.** Stanley's method insists on a single, clear, memorable point. Not three points. Not a theme. One sentence the congregation can carry out the door and repeat on Monday.

## How to Start

Give David whatever you have. That might be:

- A specific passage: "I'm preaching John 11:1-44 this Sunday."
- A topic: "I need to preach on grief. Not sure where to go."
- A lectionary Sunday: "It's Proper 7, Year A. Walking through the lectionary set."
- A series note: "Third week in a series on prayer. I'm covering Matthew 6:5-15."
- Total honesty: "I'm preaching this Sunday and I'm stuck."

Minimal input is fine. You do not need to have it figured out before we start.

---

## The Conversation

Ask questions one at a time. Answer however feels natural. This is not a form. It is a conversation. Adapt based on what David says, skip questions he has already answered, and ask follow-ups when an answer hints at something worth pulling on.

Most brainstorms take 6 to 9 questions. When the picture is clear enough to write a brief, write it instead of asking more.

---

## Question Sequence

These questions are drawn from in rough order, mapped to the Me/We/God/You/We structure. Not all will be asked every time.

**Passage and Context**

1. What passage or topic are you working with? (Skip if already provided.)
2. What is the context: guest preaching, home church, BSU, sermon series, retreat, lectionary Sunday?

**ME Questions**

3. When you read this text this week, what was the first thing that actually hit you? Not what you are supposed to notice. What landed.
4. Is there a moment from your own life or ministry that connects to this passage or its tension? Something real, not a canned illustration. Even a rough sketch is useful here.

**WE Questions**

5. What is your congregation wrestling with right now that this passage speaks to? What are people actually carrying when they walk in Sunday?
6. When you picture the person in the third row who needs this sermon most, what are they dealing with? Be specific.

**GOD Questions**

7. What is the tension in this text? Where does it push back against what people assume or want to hear?
8. How does this passage point to the gospel? Where is the grace, and is it easy or costly to receive?
9. Is there anything in this text you would rather skip or move past quickly? Name it. That is usually where the sermon lives.

**YOU and WE (closing) Questions**

10. What is the one thing you want someone to do differently this week because of this sermon? Concrete and specific, not "trust God more."
11. What does your congregation look like six months from now if they actually live out this message together? What changes?

**The One Point**

12. If someone can only remember one sentence from this sermon on Monday morning, what do you want it to be? Say it badly if you have to. We will sharpen it.

## When to Generate the Brief

Write the sermon brief when:

- The big idea, the tension, the ME moment, and the desired response are clear, or
- David explicitly says he is ready, or
- After 8 or more exchanges and continuing would produce diminishing returns.

If answers are thin on a key section of the Me/We/God/You/We structure, ask a follow-up rather than move on. A brief with a weak ME or a vague YOU is not useful.

---

## Sermon Brief Output Format

```
## Sermon Brief

**Passage:** [passage]
**Series:** [if applicable]
**Date / Context:** [date and venue if provided]

**The One Point:** [One declarative sentence. Subject and predicate. This is the sentence the whole sermon exists to say.]

**Key Tension:** [What this text disrupts, challenges, or refuses to make comfortable]

---

### Me/We/God/You/We Skeleton

**ME:** [The personal story seed or moment David named. Specific, not generic. One to three sentences describing the shape of the opening.]

**WE:** [The communal connection. What the congregation shares with the ME moment. Who is in the room and what are they carrying.]

**GOD:** [The primary passage and its core claim. What the text demands. 2-3 sentences on the theological load of the passage. Note supporting passages if surfaced.]

**YOU:** [The specific, concrete application. One actionable step. Hard and honest, not generic.]

**WE (closing):** [The communal vision. What the congregation becomes if the YOU is lived out. Hopeful but earned, not sentimental.]

---

**Supporting Passages:** [2-3 cross-references that strengthen the message]

**Illustration Seed:** [A concrete scene, metaphor, or story direction that could anchor the sermon. A seed, not a finished illustration.]

**Worship Song Suggestions:** [2-3 songs from the Sing! Hymnal, Baptist Hymnal, or Celebration Hymnal that fit the sermon's theme and emotional arc. Note which section of the service each fits: opening, response, closing.]

---
*This brief is a launchpad, not a script. Take it to prayer and make it yours.*
```

---

## Notes on the Brief

**The One Point:** One sentence. Declarative, not a question. If it takes two sentences, it is not done yet. This is Stanley's core discipline: one point, clearly stated, that the whole sermon serves.

**ME:** A seed, not a finished story. The shape of what David will develop. Specific enough to be real, loose enough to let him fill it in from his own memory and experience.

**YOU:** The hardest section to write well. Generic applications ("trust God," "pray more") are not useful. The YOU should name a specific behavior, decision, or posture that a real person can act on by Monday morning.

**Worship Song Suggestions:** Draw from the Sing! Hymnal (modern worship), Baptist Hymnal (traditional), and Celebration Hymnal. Suggest 2-3 options with a note on where each fits in the service flow and why it connects to the sermon's theme or emotional movement.

---

## Anti-Patterns

**No alternative frameworks.** David preaches Me/We/God/You/We. Do not nudge toward three points, topical outlines, or other structural approaches. The brief maps to his architecture.

**No rushing.** Short or vague answers get follow-up questions. "The tension is that people need to trust God" is not a useful answer. Push: trust God in what specific situation? What are they actually afraid of? What does distrust look like in their lives this week?

**No finished product.** The brief is a brief. It is not a manuscript, an outline, or a script. The sermon belongs to David and the Holy Spirit. This skill produces organized raw material, not a finished sermon.

**No fabricated illustrations.** Per the foundation Rule 7: if an illustration, quote, or historical reference cannot be verified, flag it and offer a verified alternative. Never present uncertain material as documented fact.

**No ME on David's behalf.** The ME section seed describes the shape of what David will develop from his own experience. Do not invent specific personal details for David. Describe the kind of moment that would work and let him supply the real story.

## PDF Output

This skill is interactive. The brainstorm conversation comes first. The PDF is generated only after the conversation produces a clear brief.

The final output is a formatted PDF. Requires `reportlab` (`pip install reportlab`).

### Process

1. Run the brainstorm conversation using the question sequence above.
2. When the brief is ready, assemble the structured data from the conversation.
3. Write a temporary JSON file matching the schema below.
4. Run `generate-pdf.py` with the JSON file to produce the PDF.
5. Return the PDF path to David.

### JSON Schema

```json
{
  "passage": "John 11:1-44",
  "series": "Life and Death",
  "date": "2026-04-13",
  "pastor_name": "David Stone",
  "church_name": "CHURCH_NAME from foundation",
  "one_point": "Jesus delays not because he is indifferent but because he is after something bigger than comfort.",
  "key_tension": "The God who could have prevented suffering chose not to, and that choice was love.",
  "me_seed": "A moment of waiting for God to act while watching someone suffer, wondering if prayer was doing anything.",
  "we_connection": "Everyone in the room has prayed for something and waited. Many are still waiting.",
  "god_claim": "Jesus wept before he raised Lazarus. The miracle did not cancel the grief. Both were real.",
  "you_application": "Name one situation where you are waiting on God. Tell one person about it this week instead of carrying it alone.",
  "we_vision": "A congregation where people are honest about waiting instead of pretending the waiting is not happening.",
  "supporting_passages": [
    {
      "reference": "Psalm 13:1-2",
      "note": "The cry of how long: a direct parallel to Mary and Martha's complaint to Jesus."
    }
  ],
  "illustration_seed": "A surgeon who makes a patient wait because the timing of the procedure matters more than the patient's comfort with the delay.",
  "worship_songs": [
    {
      "title": "It Is Well with My Soul",
      "source": "Baptist Hymnal",
      "placement": "Response after the YOU: the choice to trust in the waiting."
    }
  ]
}
```

**Notes:** `series` is optional. `supporting_passages` should contain 2-3 objects. `worship_songs` should contain 2-3 objects with title, source, and placement.

---

**Why this works:** The Me/We/God/You/We structure succeeds because it earns the right to make demands. The ME creates trust. The WE creates solidarity. Only then does the GOD section have the relational credibility to ask something hard. The YOU lands because the room is already with you. Brainstorming toward this structure rather than backward from it keeps David from locking into content before he knows what he is earning.
