# Small Group Discussion Questions & Bible Study Facilitation

Sunday's sermon becomes Monday's small group conversation. A lectionary passage becomes a Friday night community study. A book of the Bible becomes a semester-long BSU curriculum. This skill produces the full facilitation package -- not just questions.

**Requires:** pastor-foundation skill

---

## Step 0: Clarify the Lane and Context

Before writing anything, ask for or determine:

**Lane** (required):
- **Lane 2** -- Curriculum session: a structured lesson with full leader content, participant handout, and teachable arc. Used for a class format where David is often teaching or a prepared leader is guiding.
- **Lane 3** -- Small group leader guide: a facilitation-focused guide with timed sections, discussion prompts, embedded quote markers, leader notes, and handoff capability. Used when a leader (not always David) is running a group and needs enough scaffolding to lead well.
- **Discussion-only** -- A lighter set of 8-10 questions to extend a Sunday sermon, formatted for quick use without full leader prep sections.

**Context** (required):
- Setting: Sunday school class / Friday night community group / BSU college students / athletics chaplaincy / other
- Audience: Adults (general), mixed college students + adults, BSU freshmen/upperclassmen, student athletes, other
- Is this a standalone session or part of a series?
- Will someone other than David be leading? (If yes, lean toward heavier leader scaffolding.)
- Passage or sermon being studied

**Helpful but not required:**
- Sermon notes, outline, or transcript (for sermon-follow guides)
- How much time the group has (default: 60 minutes)
- Whether a participant handout is needed
- Date and church/organization name

---

## Step 1: Extract the Core

Regardless of lane, before writing any content, identify:

1. **The Big Idea.** One sentence. Not the topic -- the point. "God is faithful" is a topic. "God's faithfulness in the wilderness is the reason you can trust him in yours" is a big idea. The Big Idea is preachable, discussable, and memorable.

2. **2-3 Key Themes.** The threads the passage or sermon returns to. These anchor the interpretation and application sections.

3. **The Primary Movement.** What should participants walk away believing differently, doing differently, or understanding more deeply?

4. **Key Scholars or Commentary Sources.** Pull from David's preferred list. For NT: Moo, Gorman, Wright, Bailey, Bock, Green. For OT/Psalms: Kidner, Brueggemann, Thompson. For preaching/pastoral application: Stott, Wiersbe. Use ESV for research. Use NLT for reading aloud in the guide.

5. **Potential Quotes.** Mark any quotes from commentators, theologians, or preachers that could enrich the teaching moment. Use INSERT QUOTE HERE as a placeholder if the exact quote is not verified. Never fabricate quotes or attribute unverifiable statements to named people. See Factual Integrity rules in pastor-foundation.

## Step 2: Build the Appropriate Output

---

### LANE 2 -- Curriculum Session

Produce a complete session document in this structure:

---

**SESSION TITLE:** [Title that reflects felt-need or life application, not just the passage reference]
**Passage:** [Reference, NLT]
**Series Context:** [If part of a series, where this falls]
**Big Idea:** [One sentence]
**Session Goal:** [What participants will understand, believe, or be able to do by the end]

---

#### Leader Preparation Notes

*(This section is for the leader only, not read aloud or shared with participants.)*

**Exegetical Background:**
[2-4 paragraphs of substantive background: historical context, literary context within the book, key word studies, what the original audience would have heard. Write as a knowledgeable friend explaining the passage, not as an academic paper. NLT for any quoted text in leader guides; ESV for any technical study notes in brackets.]*

*Example scholarly voices to draw from where relevant: Brueggemann or Thompson for OT narrative/poetry; Kidner for Psalms; Moo, Gorman, or Wright for Paul; Bailey for culturally grounded Gospel study; Stott or Wiersbe for pastoral application framing. Attribute correctly. Mark uncertain quotes INSERT QUOTE HERE.*

**Voices and Quotes:**
[2-4 quote blocks, each formatted as:]

> INSERT QUOTE HERE
> -- [Author name, source title if known]

*[Brief leader note on how/when to use this quote in the session]*

---

#### Session Facilitation Guide

**OPENING** *(5-7 minutes)*

*[Icebreaker or entry question -- accessible to anyone, no Bible background required. Draws people into the emotional or experiential territory of the passage before reading it. Give the leader two options: one lighter, one more reflective.]*

Option A (lighter): [Question]

Option B (reflective): [Question]

*Leader note: Pick one based on your group's energy. If people are quiet, start with Option A. Don't rush this -- the opening sets the tone.*

---

**PASSAGE READING** *(2-3 minutes)*

Read [passage reference] aloud from the NLT. Ask someone to read, or read it yourself. If the group is large, consider reading it in sections with different voices.

*[Brief leader note on anything to watch for as the passage is read: a shift in tone, a key phrase that will anchor later discussion, a character worth noticing.]*

---

**OBSERVATION** *(8-10 minutes)*

*What does the text actually say? These questions keep the group in the passage before jumping to meaning or application.*

1. [Observation question -- grounded in what's literally on the page. Characters, sequence, repeated words, structure. Anyone who just heard the reading can answer this.]

2. [Second observation question -- something easy to miss. A detail, a phrase, a shift in tone. Builds careful reading habits without requiring commentary.]

*Leader note: The goal here is simply to get people looking at the text. Resist the urge to explain too much. Let the group notice things.*

---

**INTERPRETATION** *(15-18 minutes)*

*What does the text mean? Move from observation into understanding.*

3. [Why the author made a specific choice -- word selection, who he addressed, structure. Moves into meaning.]

4. [Cultural, historical, or literary context that deepens understanding. Frame it as "In that world..." or "In this book, this word carries the meaning..." Keep it accessible, not academic.]

5. [How this passage fits the larger biblical story -- a thread to another text, a theme in redemption history, or how this moment contributes to the arc.]

*Key Teaching Moment: [1-2 sentences for the leader about the central insight the group should land on before moving to application. This is where the leader can bring in a quote, a brief illustration, or a sentence of their own commentary. Mark quote opportunities INSERT QUOTE HERE.]*

---

**APPLICATION** *(10-12 minutes)*

*What does the group do with this? Move from understanding into response.*

6. [Personal without being invasive. Invites honest reflection without requiring public confession. "Where do you find yourself in this..." style.]

7. [Connects the passage to daily life: work, family, relationships, finances, integrity, decisions. Makes the text relevant to Tuesday morning, not just Sunday.]

8. [A challenge question with some edge. Not harsh, but not soft. The text made a demand. This question reinforces it.]

---

**CLOSING DIRECTION** *(3-5 minutes)*

**Prayer Prompt:** [Specific direction based on the session theme. Not "pray for each other." Something like: "Spend a few minutes praying specifically about the place in your life where this passage pushed back on you. Ask God for the courage to act on what you heard, not just to think about it."]

**Response Step:** [One concrete, specific action step for the week. Specific enough to be doable. Applicable enough that it works for different life situations. Something a group member could briefly report back on next week.]

---

**Quick Leader Notes:**

*[3-5 short bullets with context-specific guidance. Examples:]*
- *If this is a college group, students may push back on [X]. Be ready with [brief framing].*
- *If this is a mixed-age group, the application to parenting will land with some; redirect toward [broader principle] for others.*
- *The most common rabbit trail from this passage is [X]. Acknowledge it, then redirect with: "That's worth its own conversation -- for today, let's stay with what the text says about [Y]."*
- *Handoff note: If another leader runs this session, the Key Teaching Moment and Voices & Quotes sections are the most important to review in advance.*

### LANE 3 -- Small Group Leader Guide

Produce the same structure as Lane 2 above, with these differences:

- **Lean heavier on leader scaffolding.** The leader may not have deep Bible background. Write the Exegetical Background to be read and absorbed, not just skimmed.
- **INSERT QUOTE HERE markers are primary.** Rather than leaving quotes blank, provide the context so the leader knows what kind of quote to look for or when to ask David to fill in the quote.
- **Handoff language throughout.** Add brief notes throughout in italics: *[If you're handing this to a leader: this is the most important section to read before the session starts.]* Style.
- **Timing is written into every section.** Every section header includes suggested time in parentheses.
- **Participant handout is always included** (see Participant Handout section below).

## Context Awareness by Setting

**Sunday school class at Sunshine Baptist** -- Lean Lane 2. David is often teaching or a trusted class leader is teaching. Commentary depth is welcome. Adults 30s-70s. Connect application to family, work, and long-term discipleship.

**Friday night community group (mixed college + adults)** -- Lane 3. Ages 18-65 in the same room. Application questions need to work across life stages. Icebreakers should bridge the gap. Exegetical background should be accessible without being dumbed down.

**BSU (Missouri State Baptist Student Union)** -- Lane 2 or Lane 3 depending on whether David is teaching or handing off to a student leader. Two audiences: students in discipleship, and ministry leaders David coaches. Freshman curriculum: accessible, identity-forming, essentials-focused. Upperclassmen/leaders: deeper, equipping-focused.

**Athletics chaplaincy** -- Lane 3, lean light on academic content. Strong on story, metaphor, and direct application to performance, competition, team, identity, pressure. Translation: NLT always. Short timed sections. Direct application questions.

## Design Rules

**Big Idea first, always.** Every question in the guide is in service of the Big Idea. If a question doesn't connect to it, cut it.

**Observation before interpretation before application.** This is non-negotiable. Groups that skip to application skip understanding the text. The inductive flow is the skill, not just a format.

**Open-ended only.** No question answerable with yes or no. If it can be answered in one word, rewrite it.

**NLT for reading aloud.** ESV for research notes and bracketed technical commentary in leader prep. Never mix translations in the same sentence of a question.

**INSERT QUOTE HERE is a feature, not a placeholder.** It signals to the leader that a strong scholarly or pastoral voice belongs at this moment. It also reminds David to fill in the right quote when he reviews before use. Never fabricate quotes. Mark uncertain attributions clearly.

**Timed sections are not optional for Lane 3.** A leader who has never led a group before needs time markers. A leader who has led for years will adjust on the fly. Write for the newer leader.

**Handoff-ready for Lane 3.** Every Lane 3 guide should be usable by someone who wasn't in the room when it was planned. The leader prep section is the key. If the leader reads only one section, it should be that one.

**Orthodox sources only.** No LDS/Mormon, Jehovah's Witness, Word of Faith, or heterodox commentary. Same Rule 3 from pastor-foundation.

**No em dashes anywhere** in any deliverable.

**Translation note on participant handout:** If printing, use NLT. If the group is diverse in translations, just reference the passage and let people use their own.

---

## Anti-Patterns

**Writing for seminary students.** If a question requires knowing Greek or having read multiple commentaries to engage meaningfully, rewrite it. Questions serve the group, not the pastor's library.

**Leading questions with one right answer.** "Don't you think God was testing Abraham here?" is a quiz with pressure, not a discussion question. Write questions that genuinely invite different responses.

**Feelings-only questions.** "How did this passage make you feel?" on repeat is not Bible study. Feelings are one lane. Observation and interpretation keep the group in the text.

**Assuming everyone heard the sermon.** Some group members skip Sunday. Every question should work on its own. The guide functions as a standalone document even for someone who walks in cold.

**Generic closing prayers.** "Pray for each other's needs" is a guide that ran out of ideas. The prayer prompt connects directly to what the passage demanded.

**Too many application questions.** Three application questions maximum. More and the group feels like a confession booth. Let the text do the work first. Application lands harder when it's earned.

**Skipping the participant handout for Lane 3.** If a leader is running the group, participants need something in their hands. Don't skip it.

---

## Output Quality Check

Before delivering any guide, verify:

- Big Idea is one sentence, comes from the text or sermon, not from a general theme
- All questions are open-ended (no yes/no answers possible)
- Observation, Interpretation, Application flow is in order
- At least one application question connects to daily work, family, or relationships
- INSERT QUOTE HERE markers are placed where a strong scholarly or pastoral voice belongs
- No fabricated quotes or unverifiable illustrations
- NLT is specified for reading aloud
- Prayer prompt is specific to the session theme, not generic
- Response step is concrete and actionable
- Participant handout is present for Lane 2 and Lane 3
- Timed sections are present for Lane 3
- Leader notes throughout for Lane 2 and Lane 3
- No em dashes anywhere in any deliverable

---

## PDF Output

The final deliverable is a formatted PDF, not terminal text. Requires `reportlab` (`pip install reportlab --break-system-packages`).

For Lane 2 and Lane 3, produce **two PDFs**: one for the leader (full guide) and one for participants (handout only).

### Process

1. Generate the guide using the steps above.
2. Assemble the structured data into the JSON schema below.
3. Write a temporary JSON file matching the schema.
4. Run `generate-pdf.py` with the JSON file to produce the PDF.
5. Return the PDF path to David.
